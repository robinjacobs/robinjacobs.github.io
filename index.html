Robin Jacobs

I'm a robotics engineer working on learning-based, dexterous manipulation at Hexagon Robotics. In January 2024, I completed my Master of Science in Robotics, Systems and Control (RSC) from ETH Zurich. Until June 2024, I was doing my civilian service in the Research in Orthopedic Computer Science Group (ROCS), where I assisted in the development of camera-based surgical navigation systems.

I am broadly interested in the intersection of robotics and machine learning. I am particularly excited about how to combine novel learning-based methods for computer vision and control to enable robots to interact with the world in a more intelligent and autonomous way. This necessitates the need for safe and robust algorithms that can be deployed on real-world systems, bringing the promise of intelligent robotics to everyday life.

Using Reinforcement Learning For Grasping Under Uncertainty

Master Thesis, RSL ETH Zurich 2023

The project focuses on using reinforcement learning and force sensor measurements to improve the robustness of grasping objects in the presence of uncertainties in the object to grasp. We explore the use of privileged teacher student learning and recurrent neural networks to encode belief states of the object's position. We deploy the learned policies to a real-world manipulation plattform and highlight the benefits of using force-based measurements to improve grasping success rates in the presence of pose uncertainties.

Approximate Predictive Control Barrier Function

Semester Project, IDSC ETH Zurich 2022

Safety filters relying on Model Predictive Control (MPC) have proven to be a powerful tool for assessing the safety of control inputs generated by learning algorithms. However, the optimization problem is often computationally expensive, hindering the application of these methods in real-world settings. This work proposes a novel safety filter which leverages a neural network-based approximation of the optimal value function from an MPC-based safety filter. By using the fact that the approximation is derived from a control barrier function (CBF), we show that safety verification is still guaranteed. Our approach offers a significant computational advantage compared to previous state-of-the-art safety filters, paving the way for real-world deployment.
